Connecting Action Semantics and Human Motion
using Fuzzy Qualitative Kinematics
Ali Asghar Manjotho, Tekie Tsegay Tewolde, Zhendong Niu
Abstract —Human motion understanding is fundamental to
various applications in computer graphics, human-robot inter-
actions, digital environments, and smart healthcare. Existing
approaches predominantly rely on quantitative and qualitative
kinematic facts for motion modeling. However, they often struggle
to establish a robust connection between motion and correspond-
ing action semantics. This is primarily attributed to the use of
inadequate motion representations and presence of uncertainties
in kinematic hierarchies. We propose a novel fuzzy qualitative
kinematics (FQK) approach to effectively bridge action semantics
and human motion. Our approach exploits the fusion of fuzzy
inference and qualitative reasoning to model expressive, complex,
and uncertain kinematic facts. First, we construct fuzzy linguistic
vocabulary and membership functions for motion attributes.
Second, we extract novel fuzzy qualitative tokens (FQTs) from
motion sequences by employing FQK to represent various motion
attributes across different motion components. Third, we propose
a dual-branch adaptive neuro-fuzzy inference (D-ANFI) block.
The D-ANFI block learn two data representations in parallel: the
fuzzy inference module learns membership functions and rule-
base, while DNN module learns the motion features. Thereafter,
the feature fusion module combines the two representations to
effectively represent the motion semantics. Forth, we demonstrate
the effectiveness of the proposed approach by integrating FQTs
and D-ANFI block a unified unified motion understanding
system to account various human motion tasks, including motion
interpolation, prediction, and generation. Finally, we introduce
expressive and intuitive kinematic text prompts to perform fine-
grained text-to-motion translation based on FQTs. Extensive
experiments on HumanML3D and KIT-ML benchmark datasets
validate the superiority of our approach over existing schemes.
Index Terms —Human motion generation, automated anima-
tions, motion semantics, fuzzy reasoning, qualitative kinematics.
I. I NTRODUCTION
HUMAN motion understanding is integral to nu-
merous applications, including human-robot interac-
tion, automated computer animations, social humanoid in
augmented/virtual reality, intelligent non-playing characters
(NPCs) in video games, physical fitness, sport analysis, and
digital film production [1]–[3]. The fundamental challenge
lies in establishing a meaningful connection between the raw
motion space and action semantic space [4]. Conventionally,
motion is represented as a sequence of 3D poses [5] or SMPL
model parameters [6], [7], while action semantics are charac-
terized by action categories or textual descriptions in natural
language. Recent advances in the field have predominantly

and action semantics presents challenges when attempting
direct mapping. However, intermediate representations have
shown promising progress in addressing the semantic gap
through the use of quantitative and qualitative kinematics.
Quantitative kinematics relies on real-valued pose geometry to
model spatio-temporal dependencies in the underlying motion.
In contrast, qualitative kinematics employs linguistic symbols
as kinematic facts to express the relationships between the
spatial and temporal components of motion.
Recently, qualitative representations of human motion have
been identified as a promising solution for conveying effective
kinematic cues [13]. Different qualitative schemes have been
proposed, including pose tokens [10], [11], motion descrip-
tors [12], global/local motion cues [14]–[16], and kinematic
phrases [4]. These approaches employ boolean kinematic
facts to encode motion semantics, such as ” Is the right foot
behind the left foot? ” or ” Is the right leg bent? ”. However,
these representations are limited in expressing human skele-
ton geometry. Moreover, they often neglect simple motion
dynamics (such as amplitude and velocity changes) and fail
to account for inherent uncertainties in the underlying motion.
Consequently, connecting action semantics and raw motion
remains a persistent challenge.
To this end, we propose a fuzzy qualitative kinematics
(FQK) approach to establish robust connections between raw
motion and action semantics. Our approach integrates qual-
itative reasoning with fuzzy logic to offer fuzzy qualitative
tokens (FQTs) as plausible intermediate motion representation.
FQTs concentrate on fuzzy uncertain kinematic facts that
are typically overlooked by general action semantics, such
as ”left hand moves forward slightly then moves backward
quickly ” or ” right leg is slightly bent inwards and the knee
is closer to the right hip ”. Our approach adopts fuzzy kine-
matic trigonometry [17], which transforms ordinary translation
and orientation Cartesian spaces into a unit circle and uses
membership functions to represent kinematic facts as fuzzy
values. Unlike boolean motion directional changes, these fuzzy
values additionally encompass changes in motion amplitudes
and velocities. Importantly, we predetermine the linguistic
variables for kinematic facts to provide flexible fuzzy intervals
that balance motion accuracy and computational complexity.
Using FQTs as an intermediate representation, we first ex-
tend existing datasets to unify the motion knowledge base.
Subsequently, we developed a motion understanding system

II. R ELATED WORK
Human Motion Representation. An accurate representation
of human motion is critical for generating realistic motion
sequences [18]. Conventional motion is typically represented
as a sequence of static pose representations characterized
by joint positions or limb rotations. The introductionof parametric body models, as demonstrated in [6], has
enabled a more accurate depiction of the human body.
Besides, a number of methods have also explored the
use of intermediate motion representation. For instance,
”posebits” were introduced in [10] as boolean pose tokens to
capture the geometric relationships between different body
joints. These posebits were extracted using joint distances,
articulation angles, and joint relative positions. Another
approach, ”posescript,” was proposed in [11] as an extension
to posebits, which employs fine-grained categorical values
to extract relationships between different body parts and
represent motion semantics in a natural language. In [12],
SDQIO was proposed as a feature set for representing
action semantics, capturing five features: steered, diversion,
quadrangular, intricacy, and obscurity. Furthermore, [14]
represented motion as aggregated local and global motion
features extracted through multi-path displacement estimation
and motion cues. Long-range dependencies were extracted
using human motion trajectories and graph grouping in [15],
[16] and were finally fused to obtain rich motion cues.
Phase functions [19] and learned motion words [20] were
also explored, although limited to specific actions such as
locomotion and dancing. Notably, [21] extended posebits
to propose rich categorical kinematic phrases (KPs) as
abstract, interpretation, and general motion representations.
Although KPs hold richer semantics, they lose much of the
semantics when translating joint orientations and positions
into categorical descriptors using thresholding. Hence,
in contrast to thresholding, we propose fuzzy qualitative
tokens to extract rich motion semantics by employing fuzzy
membership functions and qualitative kinematic trigonometry.
Human Motion Generation. Human motion generation aims
to produce realistic motion sequences for individuals or in-
teractions involving humans and objects. Existing methods
for motion generation can be categorized as regressive and
generative [22]. Regressive methods involve learning direct
mapping using input features along with a conditional signal,
typically through a supervised technique. In contrast, gener-
ative methods aim to learn the conditional joint distribution
of motion sequences in an unsupervised manner. In this
context, a range of generative models have been proposed, in-
cluding variational autoencoders (V AEs) [23]–[25], generative
adversarial networks (GANs) [26], [27], denoising diffusion
probabilistic models (DDPMs) [28], [29], normalizing flows
[30], [31], and motion graphs [32], [33]. Typically, the human
motion generation task is conditioned on various factors such
as text [34], [35], audio [36], [37], scene [38], [39], kinematic
control [40], or another motion sequence [41]–[43]. Other
works [44], [45] try to synthesize the motion by re-targeting
motion styles between different kinematic structures.

H. Motion Understanding via FQTs
To rigorously evaluate our FQT approach, we applied it to
various motion understanding tasks, including human motion
interpolation, prediction, and generation. For these tasks, we
adopted a unified approach to comprehend human motion by
utilizing a single model to learn the motion semantics to infer
all three tasks.
Motion Interpolation. Consider an arbitrarily masked pose
sequence Mm∈RT×K×3, where Tdenotes the sequence
length and Jis the number of joints describing each pose. The
objective is to recover the missing regions ˆMuand generate a
full consecutive sequence ˆM={Mm,ˆMu}. During training,
the masked pose sequence was obtained by applying a mask
to the groundtruth as Mm=MSK ⊙M. Where Mu= (1−
MSK )⊙Mis the groundtruth complementary region and
MSK refers to the binary mask applied to the sequence.
Motion Prediction. Given a partially observed motion Mi
in a pose sequence, the goal is to synthesize a plausible and
realistic pose sequence ˆM={Mi,ˆMu}without losing prior
knowledge of the input key frames.
Motion Generation. Given a text t∈ {W1, . . . , W N}as a
sequence of Nwords, the objective is to predict the motion
sequence ˆMcorresponding to t.
I. Unified Model Structure
Model Structure. Inspired by [48], we adopted a unified
approach for motion understanding tasks, as shown in
Fig. 4. Our model employs a transformer-based variational
autoencoder (V AE) network together with self-attention,
cross-attention, and modal-specific learnable distribution
tokens. Initially, we randomly mask the groundtruth motion
Mand fuzzy qualitative tokens Falong with the curriculum
learning strategy [49]. The strategy gradually increases the
masking ratio and encourages the model to learn the missing
components effectively. Randomly masked motion Mmsk and
masked tokens Fmsk are then provided to the two encoders.

IV. I MPLEMENTATION DETAILS
We trained our model for 5K epochs using a minibatch size
of 32. The network weights were initialized randomly and
optimized using an AdamW optimizer. The initial learning
rate was set to α= 10−5and decayed gradually with the
Step LR scheduler with a step size of 8 and decay rate
of 0.99. Moreover, the penalizing hyperparameters were
empirically set to λKL= 10−4andλE= 10−5. Our
transformer encoders and decoders consist of 6 layers for
each of the three encoders and decoder. Further, the latent
space dimension dwas chosen as 512. No data augmentation
techniques were applied to any of the dataset. We utilized
HumanML3D and KIT-ML training sets for training and
held out test sets for validation on a single NVIDIA
3080 Ti GPU. All the sequences were resampled at a rate of
15Hz with the sequence length ranging from 20 to 225 frames.
FQT-intervened Motion Interpolation. First, the incomplete
motion sequence Mmis passed as a masked sequence to obtain
two distribution variables: zf=Ef(Mm)andzm=Em(Mm).
The interpolated motion is then predicted by the motion
decoder as ˆM=Dm(zf, zm).
FQT-intervened Motion Prediction. Motion prediction in our
model works similarly to interpolation, with the exception that
all masked poses follow the observed poses in the sequence.
FQT-intervened Motion Generation. First, the given text t
is passed through the text encoder to obtain the distribution
variable zt=Et(t). We then pass the entirely masked motion
sequence to the motion encoder to obtain another distribution
variable, zm=Em(Mm). The generated motion is then
predicted by the motion decoder as ˆM=Dm(zm, zt).

V. K INEMATIC PROMPT GENERATION
Drawing inspiration from [4], we introduce the extended
kinematic prompt generation (KPG). It aims to improve the
interpretability and objectivity of kinematic facts within a
motion sequence. Extended KPG leverages FQTs, as opposed
to KPs, to generate motion from text prompts that include
kinematic facts as motion cues. To simplify the prompt, we
transform FQT tokens into various English verbs, adverbs,
and prepositions, representing qualitative states for distance,
angle, and velocity as textual descriptions. We constructed a
dataset of 1548 text prompts, where the FQTs were seamlessly
integrated with kinematic facts to produce a comprehen-
sive representation of motion. A subset of these prompts is
presented in Table. I for reference. To convert FQTs into
corresponding text prompts, we adopt a strategy similar to
that proposed by [4].

A. Datasets
HumanML3D. HumanML3D [46] is a comprehensive 3D
human motion-language dataset that encompasses a diverse
array of human actions, including walking, jumping, swim-
ming, playing golf, cartwheel, and dancing, among others.
Each motion clip is accompanied by three to four single-
sentence descriptions. The motions have been downsampled to
20 frames per second (fps), with each clip duration spanning
from 2 to 10 seconds. In total, the dataset contains 14,616
motions and 44,970 descriptions, featuring a vocabulary of
5,371 distinct words. The total length of motions amounts to
28.59 hours. The average motion length is 7.1 seconds, while
the average description length is 12 words. HumanML3D data
adheres to 22 joint SMPL skeleton structure.
KIT Motion-Language (KIT-ML). KIT-ML [47] comprises
3,911 human motion sequences complemented by 6,278 tex-
tual descriptions. The total vocabulary size, excluding capital-
ization and punctuation, is 1,623 words. Each motion sequence
is accompanied by one to four sentences with an average
description length of approximately 8 words.

Accuracy. The accuracy of a model measures how well the
generated samples align with the expected action label or text
description, particularly in text-to-motion tasks. It provides a
broad overview of how closely the generated motions match
the corresponding texts.
R-Precision. R-Precision is a measure of the alignment be-
tween the generated motions and their accompanying descrip-
tions. This is determined by evaluating the relevance of the
top-k ranked items. The precision at the first, second, and third
ranks is referred to as top-1, top-2, and top-3, respectively.
The R-Precision metric calculates and ranks the Euclidean
distances between the features and then averages the accuracy
of the top-k results, providing a detailed assessment of the
consistency between text and motion.

E. Ablation Study
To assess the effectiveness of the proposed method and its
constituent modules, we conducted an ablation study. Various
combinations of FQT sets have been adopted to assess the
potential of each FQT in conveying action semantics. We
setup following four FQT sets:
F1.considers FJAT, FPAT, FJDT, and FRDT.
F2.considers FDT, FPRDT, and FPDT.
F3.considers FLAT, FLOT, and FJVT.
The results of the ablation study are presented in Ta-
ble. IV. The experiments reveal that optimal performance is
achieved when using the union of all FQT sets, resulting
in an accuracy improvement of 28.19%. It is worth noting
that pose-centric tokens, including FJAT, FPAT, FJDT, and
FRDT, contribute significantly in conveying action semantics
compared to motion-centric tokens such as FDT, FPRDT,
FPDT, FLAT, FLOT, and FJVT. When used independently,
the pose-centric tokens provide an improvement in accuracy
of 13.42%. Furthermore, the angular and velocity tokens,
including FLAT, FLOT, and FJVT, contribute better than FDT,
FPRDT, and FPDT. This underscores the crucial role of FQTs
in conveying action semantics and providing effective motion
representation.

VIII. L IMITATIONS AND FUTURE WORK
While fuzzy qualitative kinematics presents improved
representations of intermediate motion, there is still room
for improvement. (1) Increasing the fuzzy vocabulary by
expanding the number of overlapping fuzzy intervals may
compromise the intuitiveness of textual prompts. This
expansion complicates the learning process by widening the
text prompt space. (2) Including additional motion attributes
beyond distance, angle, and velocity has the potential to
further enrich the semantics of fuzzy qualitative kinematics.
